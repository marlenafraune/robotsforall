AAAI Fall Symposium 2026

Accessibility Guidelines for Human-Robot Interaction

A 2.5-day symposium bringing together researchers, practitioners, and disability community members to advance accessibility guidelines for robots — and build the community to sustain this work.

November 5–7, 2026
Westin Arlington, Arlington, VA
~40–60 participants
Express Interest Learn More

The problem

Robots are already in the world. Accessibility guidelines are not.

Robots are operating in public spaces, hospitals, workplaces, and homes — and they are already causing harm to people with disabilities. Delivery robots block curb cuts. Hospital telepresence systems exclude patients with visual or motor disabilities.

Yet no comprehensive accessibility standards exist for physically embodied robots. Current frameworks like WCAG do not address the unique challenges they pose. This symposium exists to change that.

Our approach

Disability-led, research-grounded, community-built.

This symposium is expressly led by and centered on the disability community. Organizers and a majority of confirmed participants have disabilities. Disability community members participate as co-equal contributors alongside academic researchers.

Our long-term goal is to establish robot accessibility guidelines as the WCAG of robots — a global standard for human-robot interaction, shaped by and for the disability community, capable of informing ADA standards and technology policy worldwide.

Symposium objectives

What we aim to accomplish

The symposium is structured around three interconnected goals, each building on the last across 2.5 days.

01

Share and align on current knowledge

Through keynote talks, participant presentations, and structured discussion, participants build a shared foundation and identify where the field agrees and where open questions remain.

02

Advance robot accessibility guidelines

Working from an existing draft framework, participants identify recommended changes, prioritize critical gaps based on disabled people's needs, and produce actionable guideline updates.

03

Build community and develop future directions

Structured sessions for workshopping research in progress, forming collaborative teams, and developing joint grant proposals — with the goal of sustaining this community beyond the symposium.

Symposium format

How the symposium is structured

The 2.5-day format is designed to maximize community building and tangible output, not passive presentation. Details will be confirmed upon acceptance.

01
Share and align on current knowledge

Keynote Talks

Invited speakers from lived experience of disability, research, and industry, sharing perspectives that ground and orient the symposium's work.

Participant Talks

Academic and disability community members present submitted work — including lightning talks and in-depth presentations — on topics related to accessibility, robots, and HRI.

02
Advance robot accessibility guidelines

Guideline Development Sessions

Small-group sessions to review, critique, and extend the draft PHRIAG framework — organized by guideline principle and led by participants with relevant expertise.

03
Build community and develop future directions

Collaboration Development

Structured sessions for forming research teams, brainstorming collaborative projects, and drafting joint grant proposals.

Community Building

Ample time for structured conversation throughout all 2.5 days, including poster sessions and speed networking.

Who should attend

This symposium is for you if...

We welcome a broad range of participants — from researchers to practitioners to community members. Details on how to express interest will be shared upon acceptance.

Researchers in HRI, AI, or HCI

Working on accessibility, usability, or inclusive design of robots or AI systems — at any career stage.

Accessibility researchers

With expertise in digital or physical accessibility, assistive technology, disability studies, or related fields.

Disability community members

With lived experience of disability who want to shape how robots are designed — no academic affiliation required.

Industry practitioners

Building or deploying human-facing robots and seeking research partnerships and accessibility guidance.
